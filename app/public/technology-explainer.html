PDF Parsing
a.k.a. "reading the document"
Core concept

A PDF file isn't like a Word document. When you open a PDF, you see words on a page — but underneath, the file is actually a list of instructions: "draw this letter at position X, Y in this font at this size." There's no concept of a "sentence" or a "paragraph" built in.

Parsing is the process of reading those raw drawing instructions and reconstructing the text, positions, and formatting that you see on screen. Think of it as translating blueprint coordinates back into a readable floor plan.

🏥
Think of it like
A lab technician reading a pathology slide. The tissue sample is there, but someone needs to examine it, identify structures, and report what they see. Parsing is that examination step — taking raw material and identifying the meaningful content.

PyMuPDF
the engine that reads your slides
Core concept

PyMuPDF is a well-established, open-source PDF reading library — think of it as the specific instrument we use to parse your documents. It's been in active development since 2012 and is used by thousands of organizations worldwide.

What makes it particularly useful for pharma slides: it can tell us not just what text says, but how it's formatted — including whether a character is a superscript. That's critical, because superscripts are how your slides link claims to references.

🔬
Think of it like
A high-resolution microscope vs. a magnifying glass. Both let you see things, but the microscope gives you precise measurements — cell size, structure, characteristics. PyMuPDF gives us precise measurements of text: exact position, exact size, exact formatting flags.

When PyMuPDF reads a page, it returns every piece of text along with its exact coordinates (where on the page), font size (how large), and formatting flags (is it superscript? bold? italic?). This is what allows the tool to distinguish a superscript "1" from a regular "1" — something your reviewers do instantly by eye, but software needs explicit data to do correctly.

→
PyMuPDF is the reader. It opens the PDF and extracts text with all its formatting details. It's not AI — it's a precision text extraction tool.
Superscript Detection
finding the reference markers
Core concept

In your slide decks, a superscript number like "1" after a statement means "this claim is backed by reference #1." Your reviewers spot these instantly. The tool spots them the same way — by looking at the font data.

When PowerPoint exports a PDF, each superscript character gets a formatting flag that says "I'm a superscript." PyMuPDF reads that flag directly. There's no guessing, no pattern matching, no AI trying to figure out if something "looks like" a superscript. The file itself says it is one.

🏷️
Think of it like
Reading a barcode vs. trying to guess what a product is by looking at it. The superscript flag is the barcode — explicit, unambiguous, machine-readable. The tool reads the label, not the appearance.

Once a superscript is found, the tool looks at what text it's attached to (the claim statement immediately to its left) and what number it contains. Then it looks in that page's footnote area or references section for the matching numbered citation.

→
Superscripts aren't detected by appearance — the PDF file explicitly marks them. The tool reads the label, just like scanning a barcode.

Text Extraction
the overall approach
How it all fits

"Text extraction" is the umbrella term for what this tool does. Rather than asking an AI to look at your slides and tell us what it thinks, the tool extracts the text directly from the file data — reading the underlying structure of the PDF, not a visual interpretation of it.

This is the same fundamental technology that lets you search for a word in a PDF, or copy and paste text out of one. The tool simply does it more precisely — extracting not just the words, but their exact positions, sizes, and formatting, so it can identify which text is a superscript, which is a footnote, and which is body content.
